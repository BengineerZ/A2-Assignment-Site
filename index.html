Subtheme: Transit-Oriented Development

Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) aims to create dense, affordable, and accessible housing near public transit hubs. The MBTA Communities Act mandates zoning changes to encourage multi-family housing near transit stations, but resistance from suburban municipalities and affordability concerns pose challenges. This report explores the relationship between transit accessibility, housing density, affordability, and socio-economic patterns in Greater Boston through a series of visual analyses.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What is the current state of Transit Development in Boston?
  2. How does TOD impact housing supply, density, and affordability in Greater Boston?
    1. What role does zoning play in promoting or hindering TOD?
    2. What is the MBTA Communities Act, and how does it promote TOD?
  3. What are the main challenges and resistance to implementing TOD in Greater Boston?
    1. Which municipalities are resistant to zoning, and where are they geographically
    2. How do municipalities with higher-income households approach zoning and TOD?
  4. How does transit access correlate with population density and housing development?
  5. What are the socio-economic patterns of TOD in Greater Boston?
    1. How does the current transit development in Boston relate to racial and socio-economic equity?
Whilst some of these big questions are only partially answered, we hope this report indicates future directions of analysis which could elucidate any of the answers to these questions. *The following report is structured to tell a compelling story, as such, the aformentioned questions are answered by multiple figures each - the report doesn't exactly follow the questions linearly, but interleaves answering as insights are uncovered!*

Discoveries & Insights

This map displays the MBTA rail network overlaid on Metro Boston municipalities, highlighting the distribution of transit access across the region. The MBTA network is dense in central Boston and its immediate surroundings, while suburban areas have more limited coverage, primarily through commuter rail. This spatial disparity has implications for housing density and future TOD, with urban cores seeing more concentrated development and suburban areas maintaining lower-density residential patterns. This figure presents the population distribution across the Metro-Boston area, which is then overlayed with the MBTA Rail lines. Once again, this overview figure makes apparent the distinct regions of Metro-Boston area, where the inner city of central Boston has the highest population, which diminishes towards the outskirts. Generally, we see boosts in population count along the MBTA lines. This makes sense, as the commuter rail is designed to ferry workers from the wider metro Boston area to the city and back. As a result, the regions surrounding MBTA lines and stations see a population increase. Although the previous two figures are meant to provide an introduction to the structure of the transit network in and around Boston, as well as the overall population distribution, we will see in the following analysis that this distribution of the MBTA network is central to the future of transit- oriented development in Boston. This figure presents the assigned minimum multi-family unit capacity as a percentage of the total housing units (as of January 2025) for MBTA Transit Communities (excluding central Boston). Overlayed on top of this are the MBTA Rail Transit Stations. This data is part of the new multi-family zoning requirements for MBTA communities, as part of a recent swathe of transit-oriented development aimed at alleviating the housing crisis in Boston and the surrounding area (MBTA Communities Act). The lack of zoning for multi-family housing is a barrier for new housing development in MA. By assigning multi-family housing near MBTA transit stops, local officials hope to improve housing availability and reduce housing costs. In addition, local amenities get a boost. Here, we see that the density and proximity to local MBTA rail transit stops correlates well with the assigned minimum multi-family unit capacity. That is to say, local policy makers are choosing to override local zoning laws and impose transit-oriented development on the Metro-Boston Municipalities. To understand how prior zoning has not improved the housing crisis, consider the following plot. This figure is a scatter plot illustrating the relationship between the number of MBTA stops in a municipality and the share of multi-family housing. Municipalities with more MBTA transit stops generally exhibit a very slightly higher proportion of multi-family housing. However, the trend is very weak and outliers such as certain commuter rail towns show low multi-family housing shares despite having multiple transit stops. This discrepancy highlights the role of (locally governed) restrictive zoning policies in limiting TOD potential. Policy interventions, such as up-zoning around these transit nodes, could help align development patterns with transit accessibility. This raises an important question: how can the new zoning requirements introduced in the MBTA Communities Act improve the current situation outlined in the figure above? This figure shows another scatter plot, this time illustrating the proposed minimum unit capacity, in relation to the number of MBTA Transit Stops within the municipality. Under the MBTA Communities Act, we would see a greatly increased correlated between the amount of new multi-family units and the number of MBTA transit stops within each municipality. That is to say, the proposed new zoning laws would work to improve housing availability and affordability in areas that need it the most. By balancing housing supply with demand (in relation to the density of close-by MBTA transit stops), policy makers hope to alleviate the housing crisis in Boston. Of course, resting control away from local municipalities and towns with regards to their own zoning is quite controversial, and has led to some resistance in certain regions. In particular, eastern municipalities like Milton, Chelsea and Rockport. Can we map resistance to TOD? To do so, we have to understand some key reasons behind why certain municipalities are against the new act. We've already mentioned loss of control, where many residents view the act as an overreach of state government. The preservation of small-town character is another major factor, where residents worry that increased housing density will alter the aesthetics and character of their community. More concrete factors include infrastructure and resource strain, where local roads, schools and transport links may fail to support significant and rapid increases in housing. Finally, residents stronger consider their economic and property value concerns. Some worry that adding lots of multi-family housing could reduce property values and increase taxes to support expanded services. We will investigate these factors in the following two figures. This figure maps the number of MBTA transit stops/stations within each metro Boston municipality, excluding central Boston. As expected, the general trend is that there are more transit stops for more central municipalities. Interestingly, for the communities which are resistant to the act, we see that they have relatively lower numbers of transit stops. For example, Milton and Chelsea, although very central, have reduced transit densities in comparison to neighbouring municipalities. Yet, under the MBTA act, they are treated as 'rapid-transit' communities, and are required to undergo significant transit-oriented development. This helps paint a picture of regions resistant to change - those that are expecting significant change as a direct result of TOD. In this figure, we illustrate the average percentage of single family households per municipal zone, compared with the percentage of high-earning households (>200k). Following the previous discussion, we note that municipalities with a high prevalence of single-family zoning also tend to have a greater proportion of high-earning households. This lends credence to the notion that wealthy central regions with higher proportions of single-family housing are the most likely to resist the Act. In other words, they are most likely to resist changes to the character of their community. In addition, this suggests that restrictive zoning policies may contribute to socio-economic exclusion, preventing lower-income populations from accessing high-opportunity areas with robust transit connections. Reforming zoning laws to allow more multi-family housing could promote economic diversity and equitable TOD development. In this figure, we map the residential development sales from 2000-2023 in the Boston city area, which are coloured based on proximity (within a 0.5 mile radius) to an MBTA Rail Station. The most active housing markets are clustered around subway lines, particularly the Red, Green, and Orange Lines. Sales activity diminishes significantly as distance from transit stations increases. This trend underscores the premium placed on transit accessibility, reinforcing the importance of ensuring affordability near key transit hubs to prevent displacement and maintain equity. This raises the question: How does the current transit development in the Metro-Boston Area relate to racial and socio-economic equity? In this figure we map the percentage of white households across Metro Boston municipalities. The racial composition of Metro Boston municipalities varies significantly, with predominantly white suburban areas often exhibiting lower housing density and stricter zoning policies. This pattern suggests that historical and systemic zoning practices may have contributed to this discrepancy, and limited housing accessibility for non-white communities. Addressing these disparities through equitable zoning and transit policies could foster more inclusive growth. Although we do not analyse historical zoning practices in this report, this is an interesting area of future work. Instead, we focus on understanding current transport equity. This figure shows a demographic breakdown of municipalities with the highest percentage of non-white households, as shown in the previous map. We focus our analysis on these communities in understanding how equitable the current transport network is, and how this may be affected by future TOD under the MBTA communities act. This scatter plot illustrates the percentage of non-white households in relation to the number of MBTA transit stops within each municipality. MBTA stations are distributed across diverse communities. There doesn't appear to be a strong correlation between the two. As a direction of future research, it would be beneficial to extract data for sub-regions of the municipalities. A finer grained analysis of localised communities and overall demographics within each municipality could reveal a stronger relationship between historically marginalised communities, and access to transport. Additionally, gentrification concerns arise in areas where TOD initiatives may drive displacement. Ensuring that TOD projects include affordability measures and community protections will be key to fostering equitable transit access. Analysing this aspect of the MBTA communities act is another interesting direction of future research.

Summary

This report examines the impact of Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) in Greater Boston, focusing on the MBTA Communities Act and its role in promoting multi-family housing near transit hubs. Our analysis reveals that while transit access generally correlates with higher population density, restrictive zoning policies in suburban municipalities have limited housing development. The MBTA Communities Act seeks to address this by mandating zoning reforms, though resistance is strongest in wealthier, predominantly single-family-zoned communities that cite concerns over local control, infrastructure strain, and property values. Additionally, while MBTA transit stops serve diverse communities, disparities in housing accessibility and affordability persist, with wealthier, predominantly white municipalities maintaining exclusionary zoning practices. Ensuring TOD initiatives include affordability measures is crucial to fostering equitable growth. Future research into localised demographic trends, gentrification risks, and the long-term effects of TOD policies could provide deeper insights into the impact of zoning reforms on housing access and socio- economic mobility. While the MBTA Communities Act represents a significant step toward alleviating Boston’s housing crisis, its success will depend on effective implementation, community buy-in, and policies that balance growth with equity.
